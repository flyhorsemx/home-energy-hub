Solar can still make sense in 2026, but the math is more local than older solar articles suggest. The broad federal 30% homeowner credit is no longer the default assumption for new projects after the 2025 deadline. That means homeowners should compare **gross installation cost**, **state and utility incentives**, **net metering or net billing**, and **financing terms** before deciding.

For the federal-credit details, see our updated [Solar Tax Credit 2026 guide](/blog/solar-rebates-incentives). This page focuses on what homeowners are likely to pay by state and how to compare quotes without relying on stale incentive assumptions.

## How Much Do Solar Panels Cost by System Size?

Solar is priced per watt installed ($/W). The national average runs $2.50–$3.80/W before incentives. Here's what that translates to by system size:

| System Size | Annual Production (est.) | Gross Cost | Good Fit |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5 kW | 6,000–7,500 kWh | $12,500–$19,000 | Small home, low usage |
| 7 kW | 8,400–10,500 kWh | $17,500–$26,600 | Average US home |
| 8 kW | 9,600–12,000 kWh | $20,000–$30,400 | Larger home, moderate EV use |
| 10 kW | 12,000–15,000 kWh | $25,000–$38,000 | Large home, EV + heat pump |
| 12 kW | 14,400–18,000 kWh | $30,000–$45,600 | High usage, battery storage |

*Production estimates assume 4–5 peak sun hours/day. Actual production varies by location, roof orientation, and shading.*

## What's Included in a Solar Installation Quote

A complete solar quote should itemize:

| Component | Typical Cost Share | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Solar panels | 25–35% | Panel brand and wattage affect price |
| Inverter(s) | 8–15% | String vs. microinverter vs. power optimizers |
| Mounting hardware | 5–10% | Roof type affects complexity |
| Electrical (wiring, conduit) | 8–12% | Panel upgrades are extra |
| Labor | 20–30% | Varies significantly by market |
| Permitting and interconnection | 3–7% | Jurisdiction dependent |
| Monitoring system | 1–3% | Often included |

If a quote is suspiciously low, check whether it includes permitting, interconnection fees, and a production monitoring system.

## Why 2026 Solar Quotes Vary So Much

### System size

Most homes need a system between 6 kW and 10 kW. Larger homes, EV charging, heat pumps, or electric water heating can push the system larger. Getting sized correctly is critical — many installers upsell oversized systems.

### Roof complexity

Steep roofs, tile roofs, multiple roof planes, shade, structural work, or an old roof can raise the installed cost. If a roof replacement is likely within the next few years, price roof work before installing panels.

### Utility export rules

Full retail net metering creates a very different payback than avoided-cost export credits. In markets with weak export compensation, batteries may improve self-consumption, but they also add cost.

### State and utility incentives

In 2026, local incentives matter more. Ask each installer which programs apply to your ZIP code and whether pre-approval is required before installation.

Batteries add cost and affect payback period. They make the most financial sense in markets with weak net metering export rates (where self-consumption has more value) or frequent outages where backup power matters.

## What to Ask Every Solar Installer

1. What is the gross installed cost before incentives?
2. Which incentives are included, and are they guaranteed or estimated?
3. Are you assuming a federal tax credit? If yes, what current IRS source supports it?
4. What export rate are you using for excess power?
5. What is the Year 1 production estimate in kWh?
6. Does the quote include panel, inverter, labor, roof, and workmanship warranties?
7. What happens if interconnection or rebate approval is delayed?
8. Is monitoring included, and for how long?
9. What is your installer certification and license number?

## Lease vs. Buy vs. Loan

| Option | Upfront Cost | Who Owns Panels | Savings Potential | Flexibility |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cash purchase | Full cost | You | Highest | Full — can sell with home |
| Solar loan | $0–low | You | High | Good — paid off over time |
| Solar lease | $0 | Installer | Moderate | Limited — tied to contract |
| PPA | $0 | Installer | Low to moderate | Limited — rate locked in |

Cash or loan purchases allow you to own the system and keep all the production value. Leases and PPAs reduce upfront cost but transfer most financial upside to the installer. If you plan to stay in your home 10+ years, ownership typically produces better returns.

## Bottom Line

The best 2026 solar decision starts with comparable local quotes. Do not compare one quote that assumes outdated federal credits against another quote that uses current rules. Separate the gross cost, state or utility incentives, export-credit assumptions, and financing terms so the payback math is honest.
