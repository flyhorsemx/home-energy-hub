Mini-split systems have transformed home heating and cooling. What used to be rare technology is now the fastest-growing HVAC category in the US — and for good reason. They're efficient, flexible, and don't require ductwork.

This guide covers everything you need to know before your installation day.

## What Is a Mini-Split System?

A mini-split (also called a ductless mini-split or ductless heat pump) consists of two main components:

**Outdoor unit (condenser):** Sits outside like a traditional AC condenser. Contains the compressor, condenser coil, and refrigerant.

**Indoor unit (air handler):** Mounts on the wall, ceiling, or floor inside the room. Contains the evaporator coil and fan. Connected to the outdoor unit via refrigerant lines and electrical cable run through a 3-inch hole in the wall.

Mini-splits are heat pumps — they move heat rather than generate it, making them 2–4x more efficient than electric resistance heating.

## Single-Zone vs Multi-Zone Systems

**Single-zone:** One outdoor unit, one indoor unit. Controls temperature in one room or area.
- **Cost:** $1,500–$4,500 installed (9,000–24,000 BTU range)
- **Best for:** Single room additions, converted spaces, supplemental cooling/heating

**Multi-zone:** One outdoor unit, 2–5 indoor units. Each indoor unit can be set independently.
- **Cost:** $3,500–$12,000+ installed depending on number of zones
- **Best for:** Whole-home systems (no ductwork), multiple rooms, zoned comfort control

**Key decision point:** If you're adding climate control to one or two rooms, a single-zone makes sense. If you're replacing a whole-home system or doing a full renovation, a multi-zone can serve the entire house.

## Mini-Split System Types

### Wall-Mount (Most Common)
Mounts high on the wall, 7–8 feet up. Best airflow distribution. Easiest to install. Most common for residential applications.
- **Capacity range:** 6,000–24,000 BTU per unit
- **Installation visibility:** Visible on wall — aesthetics matter to some homeowners

### Ceiling Cassette
Mounts flush with the ceiling in a drop-ceiling or recessed in drywall. Less visible, distributes air in four directions.
- **Capacity range:** 9,000–36,000 BTU
- **Installation complexity:** Higher — requires ceiling access for mounting

### Floor-Mount
Mounts near the floor, similar to a baseboard unit. Good for rooms with limited wall space, large windows.
- **Capacity range:** 9,000–18,000 BTU
- **Best for:** Rooms with floor-to-ceiling windows, sunrooms

### Concealed Ducted Mini-Split
Hides in the ceiling or wall cavity; air delivered through small ducts to multiple registers. Least visible option.
- **Capacity range:** 9,000–24,000 BTU
- **Installation complexity:** Highest — requires concealed space for air handler

### What Adds to Installation Cost

**Electrical panel upgrade:** If your home's electrical service is insufficient (common in older homes), adding a dedicated 240V circuit costs $200–$600. If the panel itself needs upgrading, add $1,500–$3,500.

**Long line set runs:** Standard line set runs are 25–50 feet. Every 25 feet beyond standard adds $100–$300 in labor and materials (longer line sets require additional refrigerant charge).

**Difficult routing:** Running refrigerant lines through finished walls, between floors, or over long exterior distances significantly increases labor time.

**Permits:** Most jurisdictions require electrical and mechanical permits for mini-split installation. Reputable installers pull permits. Typical cost: $100–$300.

**Brand premium:** Mitsubishi and Daikin command 20–40% premiums over brands like Pioneer or Senville. The premium buys better efficiency ratings, longer warranties, and more proven reliability.

## The Installation Process (What to Expect)

### Step 1: Site Assessment (1–2 hours)
A contractor visits to:
- Assess room dimensions and layout
- Determine best indoor unit location (needs 6+ inches clearance above, 8+ inches on sides)
- Plan refrigerant line routing (shortest path to outdoor unit)
- Identify electrical requirements
- Provide written quote

### Step 2: Pre-Installation Preparation
- Contractor pulls required permits
- Equipment ordered and delivered (1–2 weeks lead time for some brands)
- Homeowner clears wall area where indoor unit will mount

### Step 3: Outdoor Unit Installation (1–2 hours)
- Mount brackets or pad on a solid, level surface (concrete pad, composite pad, or wall brackets)
- Minimum 12 inches clearance from ground; 18 inches on all sides; 24 inches in front
- Secure unit to prevent movement
- Connect electrical disconnect

### Step 4: Indoor Unit Installation (1–2 hours per zone)
- Mark and drill 3-inch hole through exterior wall (angled slightly downward to exterior)
- Mount wall bracket at correct height and level
- Route refrigerant lines, electrical cable, and condensate drain through wall
- Mount air handler on bracket
- Seal wall penetration with weatherproof foam and sleeve

### Step 5: Refrigerant Line Connections (30–60 minutes)
- Connect pre-insulated lineset between indoor and outdoor units
- Outdoor unit connection requires flare fittings — this is precision work
- Evacuate the line set with a vacuum pump (removes air and moisture)
- Open service valves to release factory refrigerant charge
- Check for leaks with electronic leak detector

### Step 6: Electrical Connection (30–60 minutes)
- Wire dedicated circuit from electrical panel
- Connect electrical cable between indoor and outdoor units
- Install outdoor disconnect box (required by code)

### Step 7: Startup and Testing
- Power on system and run in test mode
- Verify both heating and cooling operation
- Check airflow direction control and fan speed settings
- Confirm temperature differential (air exiting should be 15–20°F cooler than room temp in cooling mode)
- Walk through remote control and app setup
- Review maintenance requirements with homeowner

## Federal and State Incentives

**IRA 25C Tax Credit:** 30% of equipment and installation cost, up to **$2,000** for qualifying heat pumps. Mini-splits qualify if they meet efficiency thresholds (check ENERGY STAR database for qualifying models).

**Utility rebates:** Many utilities offer $200–$800 rebates for qualifying mini-split installations. Check your utility's website before purchasing equipment — some rebates require pre-approval.

## Getting the Best Quote

Get at least 3 quotes. Red flags in quotes:
- No site visit before quoting
- Equipment brand not specified
- No mention of permits
- Unusually low price (often means used equipment, unqualified labor, or no permits)

Green flags:
- NATE-certified technicians
- Pulls all required permits
- Specifies brand and model number in writing
- Warranty on labor (1–5 years typical)
- References and verifiable reviews
