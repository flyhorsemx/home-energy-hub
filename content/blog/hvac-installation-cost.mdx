## HVAC Cost by Home Size and Capacity

System capacity is measured in tons (1 ton = 12,000 BTU/hour). A properly-sized system requires a Manual J load calculation — not just matching the previous unit. Undersized systems run constantly; oversized systems short-cycle, cause humidity problems, and wear out faster.

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Under 1,000 sq ft | 1.5–2 ton | $3,500–$6,000 | $6,000–$10,000 | $5,000–$9,000 |
| 1,000–1,500 sq ft | 2–2.5 ton | $4,000–$7,000 | $7,000–$11,000 | $6,000–$10,500 |

## Cost Drivers

### Home size and Manual J sizing

Equipment should be sized with a Manual J load calculation. Copying the old system size can lead to short-cycling, uneven comfort, humidity problems, and wasted money. Always ask for the sizing documentation before installation.

### Ductwork condition

Duct sealing, duct resizing, returns, or full duct replacement can materially change the quote. Ductwork repairs typically add $800–$2,500; partial replacement adds $2,000–$5,000; a full duct system replacement can add $4,000–$10,000 for a typical home. A low bid that ignores ducts may not solve comfort problems.

Signs you may need ductwork work alongside HVAC replacement:
- Rooms that are always too hot or cold
- High energy bills relative to home size
- Visible duct damage in attic or crawl space
- System over 20 years old (ducts age with equipment)

### Efficiency level

Higher SEER2, HSPF2, and AFUE ratings can reduce operating cost, but the payback depends on your local utility rates and how much the system runs. As a rough guide:

| Efficiency Upgrade | Additional Equipment Cost | Typical Annual Savings | Payback |
|---|---|---|---|
| 14 → 16 SEER2 (AC) | +$400–$700 | $80–$150 | 3–6 years |
| 16 → 18 SEER2 (AC) | +$600–$1,200 | $100–$200 | 5–8 years |
| 80 → 96% AFUE (furnace) | +$400–$800 | $120–$250 | 3–6 years |
| Standard → variable-speed (HP) | +$1,500–$3,000 | $200–$400 | 6–10 years |

Efficiency upgrades pay back fastest in climates with long heating or cooling seasons and higher utility rates.

### Electrical work

Heat pumps, mini-splits, and heat pump water heaters may require electrical work. Common electrical additions:
- Dedicated 240V circuit for heat pump outdoor unit: $200–$500
- Panel upgrade (100A → 200A): $1,500–$3,500
- New disconnect box near outdoor unit: $100–$250

Ask whether panel or circuit upgrades are included in the quote or listed as a separate line item.

### Permits and warranty

Permit handling, startup documentation, registered warranties, and labor warranties should be explicit. Skipping permits can create problems during resale or insurance claims. Permit costs typically run $50–$200 depending on jurisdiction.

## Repair vs. Replace Decision

| Situation | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| System under 8 years, repair under $600 | Repair |
| System 8–12 years, repair $600–$1,500 | Get replacement quotes alongside repair estimate |
| System over 12 years, compressor failure | Replace |
| System over 15 years, any major repair | Replace |
| R-22 system with refrigerant leak | Replace (R-22 phased out, costly to recharge) |
| Low SEER system in hot/cold climate | Consider proactive replacement |

The **5,000 rule** offers a quick guide: multiply the system's age by the repair cost. If the result exceeds $5,000, replacement is usually the better investment.

## Rebates and Incentives in 2026

Federal credit rules changed after 2025. For new 2026 HVAC work, check these instead of assuming an automatic federal credit:

| Source | What to verify |
|---|---|
| Electric utility | Heat pump, mini-split, smart thermostat, duct sealing, or tune-up rebates |
| Gas utility | Furnace, boiler, thermostat, and weatherization rebates |
| State energy office | Heat pump, electrification, and income-qualified programs |
| DOE Home Energy Rebates | State-administered HOMES or HEAR availability |
| Manufacturer promotions | Seasonal discounts, financing, or equipment rebates |

Many programs require pre-approval before installation. Always ask the contractor which incentives apply to your ZIP code and whether any require pre-installation paperwork.

## What to Expect During Installation

A standard replacement (same fuel type, same location, compatible ductwork) typically proceeds like this:

1. **Site assessment** (30–60 min): Contractor measures rooms, checks ductwork, verifies electrical capacity, confirms Manual J sizing
2. **Equipment delivery**: Equipment arrives on installation day or the day before
3. **Old system removal** (1–2 hours): Refrigerant recovery, disconnect, removal
4. **New equipment installation** (2–4 hours): Outdoor unit placed, indoor unit installed, refrigerant lines connected
5. **Electrical and controls** (1–2 hours): Wiring, thermostat installation, control testing
6. **Startup and commissioning** (1 hour): Refrigerant charge verified, airflow measured, system tested in heating and cooling mode
7. **Permit inspection** (varies): Some jurisdictions require a follow-up inspection after installation

Total time for a standard replacement: **4–8 hours**. More complex installations (new ductwork, electrical upgrades, first-time heat pump conversion) may require 1–2 days.

## Quote Checklist

Before signing, make sure the quote includes:

1. Exact model numbers
2. SEER2, HSPF2, AFUE, and capacity ratings
3. Manual J load calculation
4. Ductwork inspection notes
5. Thermostat and controls
6. Permit handling
7. Disposal of old equipment
8. Labor warranty (1–2 years standard; ask what's covered)
9. Manufacturer warranty registration
10. Rebate paperwork responsibility

## Bottom Line

For most replacement projects, budget $5,000–$15,000 and compare at least three scoped quotes. The cheapest bid is often missing ductwork, permits, sizing, or warranty details. The best 2026 HVAC quote is the one that clearly separates equipment cost, labor, rebates, and assumptions — so you're comparing the same scope across all three contractors.
