Solar panels convert sunlight into electricity through a process called the **photovoltaic (PV) effect**. Here's what every homeowner should know before getting quotes.

## The Basic Science (in Plain English)

Each solar panel contains dozens of **solar cells** made from silicon. When sunlight hits these cells, it knocks electrons loose, creating a flow of electricity — this is direct current (DC) power.

An **inverter** converts that DC power into the alternating current (AC) power your home uses.

## What Happens on Cloudy Days?

Solar panels still generate electricity on cloudy days — just less of it. Modern panels are 15–22% efficient even in indirect light. Most systems are sized to produce *more* than enough in summer to offset lower winter production.

## Net Metering: Getting Paid Back

If your utility offers **net metering**, excess electricity your panels generate flows back to the grid, and you receive credits on your bill. In some states, this effectively zeroes out your bill.

<ProWarningAlert category="solar">
  Installing solar involves working on your roof and connecting to your home's electrical panel. Improper wiring can cause fires, electrocution, or void your homeowner's insurance. Always hire a licensed solar installer.
</ProWarningAlert>

## How Much Does a Solar System Cost?

The average US homeowner pays **$15,000–$25,000** for a full solar installation before incentives. After the **30% federal tax credit**, that drops to **$10,500–$17,500**.

| System Size | Avg Cost (Before Credit) | After 30% Credit |
|-------------|--------------------------|------------------|
| 5 kW        | $15,000                  | $10,500          |
| 8 kW        | $22,000                  | $15,400          |
| 10 kW       | $28,000                  | $19,600          |

## Is Solar Worth It in Your Area?

It depends on:
- Your current electricity rate (higher = better ROI)
- Hours of peak sunlight in your region
- Available state rebates and net metering policies
- Roof age and angle

The best way to know is to get a **site-specific assessment** from a local installer.
