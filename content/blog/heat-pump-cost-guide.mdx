## What Drives Heat Pump Installation Cost

**Equipment efficiency (SEER2 / HSPF2)**
Higher-efficiency systems cost more upfront but save on operating costs and earn better tax credits. Standard 15 SEER2 units run $4,000–$7,000. Premium 20+ SEER2 variable-speed units run $8,000–$14,000.

**Single-stage vs variable-speed compressor**
Variable-speed (inverter-driven) heat pumps cost $1,500–$3,000 more than single-stage units but deliver 20–35% better efficiency, quieter operation, and superior humidity control. The additional cost typically pays back in 5–7 years through energy savings.

**Existing ductwork condition**
If your ductwork is in good shape, a heat pump swap-out is straightforward. Leaky or undersized ducts require sealing or replacement, adding $500–$3,000. Homes without ducts need mini-splits (see below) or new ductwork at $5,000–$15,000.

**Electrical panel capacity**
Heat pumps run on electricity. If your panel is at capacity, an upgrade to 200A costs $1,500–$3,500. Some high-efficiency heat pumps are compatible with 240V/30A circuits already used by electric dryers.

**Regional labor rates**
Labor accounts for 30–50% of installation cost. Contractors in California, New York, and Massachusetts charge significantly more than those in the South or Midwest.

## Geothermal Heat Pump Cost

Geothermal (ground-source) heat pumps use the earth's stable underground temperature (50–60°F year-round) as a heat source and heat sink. They're the most efficient residential HVAC option — but also the most expensive to install.

**Typical geothermal costs:**
- Equipment: $3,000–$8,000
- Ground loop installation: $10,000–$20,000 (horizontal trenching or vertical bore)
- Total installed: $15,000–$30,000

**The savings case for geothermal:**
- 400–600% efficiency (vs 200–400% for air source)
- $500–$1,200/year in operating savings vs air source
- 30% federal tax credit with **no dollar cap** (vs $2,000 cap for air source)
- System lasts 25+ years (ground loops: 50+ years)

For most homeowners, air source heat pumps offer a better financial return. Geothermal makes the most sense on larger properties with available land for horizontal loops, or where installation costs are offset by exceptional energy savings.

## Heat Pump Federal Tax Credits (2025)

The Inflation Reduction Act provides two routes to heat pump tax savings:

**Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit (25C):**
- 30% of equipment and installation cost
- **Maximum credit: $2,000 per year**
- Qualifying systems must meet CEE highest efficiency tier
- Credit renews annually — you can claim it again if you add another qualifying heat pump next year

**What qualifies:** Central ducted heat pumps, mini-split heat pumps, and dual-fuel systems that meet CEE Tier 3 efficiency requirements (typically 18+ SEER2, 10+ HSPF2 for cold climates).

**State and utility rebates:**
Many states and utilities stack additional rebates on top of the federal credit:
- Massachusetts Mass Save: up to $10,000 for whole-home heat pump conversion
- New York Con Edison: $250–$1,000 per system
- California PG&E/SCE: $200–$1,000
- Maryland: $500 state rebate + utility rebates

Check your state's DSIRE database (dsireusa.org) for current programs.
