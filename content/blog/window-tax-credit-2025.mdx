If you are searching for a window tax credit in 2026, the key answer is this: **do not assume the old federal 25C window credit still applies to a new replacement project.** The federal Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit was generally limited to property placed in service by December 31, 2025.

That does not mean window incentives disappeared everywhere. It means homeowners should shift from "claim the federal credit" to "verify prior-year eligibility, utility rebates, state rebates, and whole-home weatherization programs."

## What Changed After 2025

Under the prior 25C rules, qualifying windows and skylights could receive a federal tax credit up to the annual window cap. In 2026, homeowners should treat that federal credit as expired for most new projects unless a tax professional confirms eligibility.

Older content may still say the credit runs through 2032. That guidance is stale for 2026 planning. Check the current IRS Form 5695 instructions before relying on any tax credit.

## If Your Windows Were Installed in 2025

If qualifying windows were placed in service in 2025, you may still be able to claim the credit on your 2025 return. The basic documentation checklist:

- Final contractor invoice
- Installation completion date
- Product brand and model
- NFRC label showing U-factor and SHGC
- ENERGY STAR or manufacturer certification statement
- Rebate records, if any rebate reduced your out-of-pocket cost

## Which Windows Are Worth Comparing

For comfort and efficiency, focus on the NFRC label:

| Spec | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| U-factor | Lower means less heat transfer; important in cold climates |
| SHGC | Lower blocks solar heat; useful in hot climates |
| Air leakage | Lower helps reduce drafts |
| VT | Higher allows more visible light |
| Frame material | Affects maintenance, durability, and price |

Ask each contractor to quote the same window style, glass package, frame material, installation method, and warranty so the bids are comparable.

## When Window Replacement Makes Sense

Windows are usually not the fastest-payback energy upgrade. Replacement is more compelling when:

1. Seals have failed and glass is fogging between panes
2. Frames are rotted, warped, or hard to operate
3. Drafts are causing comfort problems
4. You are replacing siding or doing exterior work anyway
5. Noise reduction, appearance, or maintenance is a priority
6. A utility or state rebate materially lowers the installed cost

For pure energy ROI, air sealing and attic insulation often come first. For comfort and curb appeal, windows may still be worth comparing.

## Contractor Questions

Before signing a window contract, ask:

1. Are these exact window models eligible for any current utility or state rebates?
2. Is rebate pre-approval required?
3. Will you provide NFRC labels and manufacturer certification documents?
4. Are labor, disposal, trim, and exterior flashing included?
5. What is the product warranty and workmanship warranty?
6. Are permits required in my city?
